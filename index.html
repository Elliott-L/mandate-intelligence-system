Core principles
One source of truth
One canonical Master Intelligence Brief. All outputs are derived views queried from it. Nothing runs from raw inputs.
Understanding before outputs
Market reality and success profile are outputs of understanding — not inputs to it. Sequencing matters.
Structured not a blob
Four strict sections A–D. Full brief always in scope. Section tags show where to anchor reasoning.
Ephemeral outputs
Outputs generated on demand. Update the brief, regenerate what you need. One thing to maintain.

Step 1 — Input collection

Gather raw material incrementally. AI tracks and categorises inputs but does not summarise, interpret, or draw conclusions. Ends when all 9 MVIs are confirmed present.

  • Company website ingested
  • Job description or role brief added
  • Hiring manager LinkedIn added
  • 2+ engineer LinkedIn profiles added
  • Funding stage and amount confirmed
  • Intake call notes or client conversation added
  • Comp range confirmed (or gap noted)
  • Interview process added — stages, people involved, their roles
  • Transcripts or social content added where available
  • Secondary inputs added where available
Gate
Do not proceed until all 9 MVIs are present. Missing inputs go into Section D as named gaps with impact stated.

Step 2 — Input audit

Critical review of input quality and completeness. Not a summary. Output is targeted questions, identified risks, and assumption audit. All gaps and conflicts feed directly into Section D.

  • Completeness assessed against MVI standard
  • Conflicting signals identified and flagged
  • Hiring context assumptions challenged
  • Comp feasibility assessed
  • Role clarity evaluated
  • Up to 10 targeted questions generated
  • Answers collected before proceeding
Gate
All high-impact questions must be answered before brief creation. Low-priority gaps are noted in Section D without blocking progress.

Step 3 — Brief creation

Build the full Master Intelligence Brief from all inputs and audit responses. Sections A through D in one session. At the end of Section B, Claude flags the success profile for client validation — that conversation happens outside the session before sourcing begins. Brief quality gate must be passed before Stage 2.

  • Section A written — facts only
  • Section B written — interpretations only
  • Section B success profile flagged for client validation
  • Client confirms or corrects success profile (outside session)
  • Section C written — search intelligence, talent landscape, CVP, funnel, failure scenario
  • Section D written — confidence, gaps, triggers
  • All inferences marked [ASSUMED]
  • Brief quality gate passed (all 9 items confirmed)
  • Brief versioned v1.0 with date
Section C — what it is and is not
Section C is search intelligence — the strategic layer that makes p6 possible. Where talent concentrates, what company types produce this profile, non-obvious pools to investigate. It is NOT a list of named companies. That list is the output of running p6, not something built into the brief.

Execute — Market reality check

Generated from the full brief. Closes with 3 immediate actions, 1 thing to stop, 1 experiment to run.

Full brief · anchors inABCD
  • Talent pool size estimated with rationale
  • Comp benchmarked vs. client expectations
  • Geographic availability assessed
  • Competing demand rated
  • Realistic funnel estimate produced
  • Search difficulty rating assigned
  • 3 actions · 1 stop · 1 experiment stated

Execute — Success profile

Generated from the full brief after client validates Section B. 60-second test mandatory before approving. If Section B fails the test, fix it before running any sourcing.

Full brief · anchors inABCD
  • Archetype description written (not a skills list)
  • Environment fit described (thrives in / fails in)
  • Specific work signals identified
  • Motivation and movement logic written
  • Strongest hook (one sentence)
  • Top 5 objections with handling
  • 60-second test applied and passed
  • Client validation confirmed

Execute — Search strategy and company targets

Translates Section C search intelligence into a named, scored, ranked company list. Section C defines where talent concentrates — p6 turns that into specific organisations. This is an ephemeral output, not part of the brief.

Full brief · anchors inABCD
  • Named companies across all tiers — not categories
  • Each company scored and ranked within tier
  • Tier 1 direct targets from Section C company types
  • Tier 2 adjacent targets from Section C adjacent pools
  • Tier 3 non-obvious targets from Section C non-obvious pools
  • Coverage self-audit — does list fully represent Section C intelligence?
  • Avoid list populated from Section C avoid profiles
  • Pipeline forecast stated
  • 3 actions · 1 stop · 1 experiment stated

Execute — Outreach by archetype

Run per archetype, not generically. Three variants per run. Full brief in context. No generic phrases in any variant.

Full brief · anchors inABCD
  • Archetype clearly defined before running
  • Platform and relationship context specified
  • Variant A — mission/problem hook
  • Variant B — technical environment hook
  • Variant C — career logic hook
  • Zero generic phrases. One specific signal per message.

Execute — Client advisory

Full brief. Honest assessments, not reassurance. Closes with ONE THING.

Full brief · anchors inABCD
  • Executive summary (2–3 sentences)
  • Market assessment with specific numbers
  • Timeline forecast (best / likely / delayed)
  • Hiring risks with likelihood and impact
  • Recommended trade-offs
  • Process recommendations
  • ONE THING close

Capture — Intelligence capture

Paste any new signal — raw and unstructured is fine. Voice note transcription, candidate message, hiring manager call notes, WhatsApp, your own observations. Claude classifies, routes, and proposes each change individually. You confirm or reject each one before it goes into the brief.

Full brief · updatesABCD
  • New signal pasted (any format)
  • Signal classified and reliability assessed
  • Any conflicts with existing brief surfaced
  • Each proposed delta confirmed or rejected explicitly
  • Section D updated
  • Downstream outputs flagged if affected
  • Version recommendation accepted or overridden
Capture vs. re-audit
Intelligence capture = proactive enrichment as new signal arrives. Re-audit = triggered by stall, wholesale spec change, or fundamental break.

Maintain — Brief re-audit

Triggered by hard decision triggers in Section D. Structural update — not routine enrichment. Closes with 3 immediate actions, 1 stop, 1 experiment.

Full brief · updatesABCD
  • Trigger condition identified
  • Search data collated (rates, rejections, objections)
  • Section A facts confirmed or corrected
  • Section B interpretations validated or updated
  • Section C intelligence assessed — did the company types and non-obvious pools described prove accurate? What needs updating?
  • Section D updated and re-versioned
  • Affected outputs regenerated
  • 3 actions · 1 stop · 1 experiment stated
Hard decision triggers
Response rate below 10% after 30 outreach → reposition · Zero to call after 2 weeks → trigger re-audit · 3+ unexplained client rejections → re-validate Section B · 2+ comp objections → escalate to client · Hiring manager changes → re-audit and re-validate Section A

Close — End-of-search debrief

Run when a search closes — hire made, paused, or cancelled. Captures what the brief got right and wrong. Takes 20 minutes. Saves hours on the next mandate.

  • Section A accuracy assessed
  • Section B interpretations validated or disproved
  • Section C intelligence validated — did the company types and non-obvious pools described prove accurate?
  • Best outreach variant identified
  • Intelligence capture gaps noted
  • What would change if starting again
  • One thing for the next search captured
Master Intelligence Brief — four sections · click to expand
Section AFactual context — confirmed, verifiable, no interpretation▾

What is objectively true. Every claim must be directly evidenced. Unevidenced claims go to Section D as inferences.

  • What the company actually does — not how they market themselves
  • Business model and revenue stage (confirmed, not estimated)
  • Funding stage, amount, and lead investors
  • Engineering team size and structure
  • Tech stack — stated and inferred
  • Product maturity — what exists vs. what is being built
  • Founder backgrounds and credibility signals
  • Role title, level, and reporting line as stated
  • Comp range as stated (or noted as unknown)
  • Location and remote/hybrid policy
  • How long the role has been open
  • Any stated hiring timeline or deadline
Section BInterpreted hiring understanding — reasoned, not raw▾

Reasoned interpretation of what the facts mean for the search. Must pass the 60-second test. Client validates the success profile via the validation flag in the Step 3 output — that conversation happens outside the session.

  • Why this role actually exists now — real reason vs. stated JD reason
  • JD deconstruction — true must-haves vs. inflated nice-to-haves
  • Skills not in the JD but actually needed
  • What the first 90 days likely involve
  • Success profile archetype — who specifically would thrive and why
  • Environment fit — what backgrounds produce success vs. failure here
  • Candidate motivators — why someone in this profile would move now
  • The strongest hook for this archetype (one sentence — mandatory)
  • Top 5 anticipated objections with handling approach
  • Competitive positioning — why choose this vs. alternatives; where this company loses
  • Client-side risks — process speed, decision-making, comp flexibility
Section CSearch intelligence — where talent lives and how to reach it▾

Strategic intelligence that informs the search. Not a list of named companies — that is the output of p6. Section C is what makes p6 possible: the reasoning about where this archetype concentrates in the market and what will move them.

  • Where this archetype concentrates — company types, stages, and cultures that reliably produce this profile
  • Non-obvious talent pools — backgrounds and environments most competitors will not look at, with rationale
  • Companies and profiles to avoid — what looks right but is wrong, and why
  • Primary sourcing channels with rationale (not just "LinkedIn")
  • Within-company targeting — which teams, functions, and seniority bands to prioritise
  • Profile signals that indicate genuine conversion potential vs. waste of time
  • CVP statement — one sentence, the single most compelling thing about this opportunity for this archetype
  • Funnel assumptions — outreach volume target and expected response rate
  • Failure scenario — one specific scenario where this search breaks and what the contingency is
Section DConfidence and gaps — what we know vs. what we are assuming▾

The honesty layer. Mandatory and never thin. Contains hard decision triggers. Updated every time new intelligence arrives.

  • CONFIRMED — facts directly evidenced from inputs
  • INFERRED — assumptions with risk rating if wrong [Low / Medium / High] — minimum 3
  • UNKNOWN — gaps that could not be filled and their impact
  • Brief confidence rating: [High / Medium / Low] with rationale
  • Highest risk gap — single gap most likely to cause search failure
  • DECISION TRIGGERS — hard thresholds that force re-audit or escalation: <10% response after 30 outreach → reposition · Zero to call after 2 weeks → re-audit · 3+ unexplained client rejections → re-validate Section B · 2+ comp objections → escalate to client
  • Re-audit trigger conditions
  • Version history — what changed between versions
Brief discipline rules
Section A = facts only. Section B = interpretation only. Section C = search intelligence — where talent lives, what moves them, what will fail. Not a company list. Section D = honest accounting of what you do and do not know. Every section must earn its place. Do not repeat ideas across sections. Update only when something meaningfully changes — then re-version.

Design rationale — what changed and why
From v1 to v2 — the core architecture change
V1 used four sequential phases producing three separate documents before execution began. V2 is two stages — Build and Execute — around one canonical Master Intelligence Brief with four internal sections. All execution outputs are ephemeral views queried from it. One thing to maintain. Nothing drifts.
V1 — three documents
Created upfront. Maintained in parallel. Prone to drift. Market reality check happened before understanding the company.
V2 — one canonical brief
Four internal sections. All outputs ephemeral. Market reality and success profile are execution outputs — not prerequisites.
v2.2 — two changes from v2.1
1. Step 3 collapsed from two passes into one prompt. The two-pass structure added friction that wouldn't survive contact with a real mandate. Client validation of Section B still happens — it just happens as a conversation outside the session, flagged in the brief output, not as a structural gate splitting the prompt library in two. 2. Section C redesigned as search intelligence, not a company list. Named companies were removed from the brief entirely — they are now the output of p6, not a brief input. Section C now holds the strategic layer: where talent concentrates, what company types and cultures produce this profile, non-obvious pools, CVP, funnel assumptions, failure scenario. p6 translates that intelligence into named companies on demand.
Why Section C is intelligence, not a company list
Earlier versions put the named company list inside the brief as Section C. This was wrong for two reasons. First, at brief creation time you have not yet done the research to produce a good named list — you are forcing output before the thinking is done. Second, the named list is ephemeral: it changes as you source, learn, and update. The brief is permanent intelligence. Section C now holds the strategic layer — where the archetype concentrates, what company types and cultures produce this profile, non-obvious pools, CVP, funnel assumptions, failure scenario. p6 translates that intelligence into named companies on demand. Brief stays clean. Output stays current.
Why intelligence capture requires confirmation, not passive absorption
Without an explicit decision layer, every new signal gets absorbed whether it deserves to or not. A candidate who passed for personal reasons can corrupt Section B's success profile. The confirmation mechanic keeps the operator in control of brief quality under live search conditions. Each proposed delta is presented individually and requires one explicit decision before it goes in.
Why hard decision triggers are in Section D
Without hard triggers, stalled searches continue because no one has defined what "stalled" means. Triggers are set when the brief is built — before the pressure of a live search makes it tempting to keep going. They are in Section D because that is where honesty lives.
Why execution closes with 3 actions, 1 stop, 1 experiment
Analysis without action is expensive thinking. Every execution prompt that produces strategic intelligence — market reality check, search strategy, re-audit — closes with three specific actions to take in 48 hours, one thing to stop, and one experiment to run. This converts intelligence into operational momentum.
The lean path
Under time pressure: Step 1 + Step 2 + Sections A and B (full) + C-lite (key company types for Tier 1 and Tier 2 — described as profiles not names, at least one non-obvious pool with rationale, CVP statement, one funnel assumption, one failure scenario) + D-lite (top 3 unknowns, confidence rating, key risk). Section D is never optional even lean. Set confidence to Low, plan a full re-audit within 5 days.
